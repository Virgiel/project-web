The Random Walker • About

Principle

Random walks can explain the observed behaviors of many processes. Its random steps are completely decorrelated from each other; this fundamental property is called the Markov process, named after the mathematician Markov. It intuitively means that at any moment, the future of the system depends on its present state, but not on its past, even the closest. In other words, the system "loses memory" as it evolves over time. The generalization takes us into the realm of Randomness.

To simulate the movement of the walker, we used Newton’s second law which states that the acceleration is proportional to the force. We can note x=x(t) the position at time t. Its velocity v = v(t) is the derivative of x and its acceleration a = a(t) is the derivative of v. We assume that the force depends only on the position and the velocity. The state of the particle at time t is described by X(t) = (x(t), v(t)) which proves that for any t, the future trajectory can be completely specified by the current state.
